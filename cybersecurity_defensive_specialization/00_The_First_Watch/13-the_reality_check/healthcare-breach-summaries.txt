BREACH SUMMARY 1: "Regional Hospital Alpha" -- Ransomware via VPN
========================================================================

Date: Q3 2023
Organization: 280-bed regional hospital, 3 clinic locations, ~1,800 staff
Sector: Acute care + outpatient services

WHAT HAPPENED:
Attackers gained initial access through a VPN appliance that had a known
vulnerability (CVE published 4 months prior, patch available, not applied).
The VPN provided direct network access to the internal server environment.
Once inside, the attackers moved laterally across the flat network, reaching
the Active Directory domain controller within 3 hours. They used a
compromised domain admin account to deploy ransomware to all Windows systems
simultaneously via Group Policy.

The ransomware encrypted 23 servers and approximately 400 workstations.
The EHR system, billing system and imaging archives were all affected.
The hospital diverted ambulances for 11 days and cancelled 340 scheduled
procedures.

WHY IT WORKED:
- The VPN appliance had not been patched despite a critical advisory from
  the vendor 4 months earlier. The IT team was aware of the advisory but
  had not scheduled maintenance.
- The internal network was flat: no segmentation between the VPN endpoint,
  servers and workstations. Once past the perimeter, the attacker had
  unrestricted lateral movement.
- No network monitoring or intrusion detection was in place. The attacker's
  3-hour reconnaissance was invisible.
- Backups were stored on a NAS device on the same network. The ransomware
  encrypted the backups along with the production systems. The most recent
  viable backup was 5 weeks old (from a monthly offsite tape rotation).
- No incident response plan existed. The response was improvised over
  11 days with external consultants brought in on day 3.

IMPACT:
- 11 days of EHR downtime (paper-based operations)
- $3.2 million in recovery costs (consultants, new hardware, overtime)
- $1.8 million in lost revenue (cancelled procedures, diverted patients)
- Regulatory investigation by HHS Office for Civil Rights
- CEO resigned 4 months after the incident
- Unknown quantity of patient data potentially exfiltrated before encryption

LESSONS CITED IN PUBLIC REPORT:
1. Patch management for perimeter devices is a survival issue
2. Network segmentation would have contained the damage
3. Backups must be isolated from the production network
4. Detection capability is not optional (hours of dwell time with zero alerts)
5. An untested incident response plan is equivalent to no plan

BREACH SUMMARY 2: "Health Network Beta" -- Insider + Credential Abuse
========================================================================

Date: Q1 2024
Organization: Multi-site health network, 5 clinics, ~950 staff
Sector: Outpatient specialty care (cardiology, orthopedics, dermatology)

WHAT HAPPENED:
A former billing department employee retained active VPN and EHR credentials
for 47 days after termination. The organization's offboarding process
required the departing employee's manager to submit a ticket to IT requesting
account deactivation. The manager forgot. IT had no automated process to
detect or disable dormant accounts.

The former employee accessed the EHR system remotely 14 times over 6 weeks,
downloading patient records for 3,211 individuals. The data included names,
dates of birth, Social Security numbers, insurance information and diagnosis
codes. The breach was discovered when a patient contacted the organization
after receiving a fraudulent medical bill.

WHY IT WORKED:
- No automated account deactivation linked to HR termination process. Account
  lifecycle depended entirely on a manual manager action.
- No MFA on VPN or EHR access. The former employee used their original
  username and password.
- No monitoring of access patterns. The account was used exclusively during
  off-hours (between 10 PM and 2 AM) from an IP address that had never been
  associated with that user. No alert was generated.
- The EHR system logged access events but nobody reviewed the logs. The audit
  trail existed but was never examined until the breach investigation.
- No DLP (Data Loss Prevention) controls on EHR data export. The former
  employee was able to download 3,211 records without triggering any alert.

IMPACT:
- 3,211 patients notified of PHI breach
- $890,000 in breach response costs (notification, credit monitoring,
  legal, investigation)
- HHS investigation and potential HIPAA fine (pending at time of report)
- Class action lawsuit filed by affected patients
- Significant reputational damage in a competitive outpatient market

LESSONS CITED IN PUBLIC REPORT:
1. Offboarding must be automated and integrated with HR systems
2. MFA is not optional for any remote access, especially to clinical systems
3. Access monitoring must include behavioral analytics (unusual hours,
   unusual volumes, unusual source IPs)
4. Logs without review are security theater
5. DLP controls on sensitive data exports are essential for insider threat

BREACH SUMMARY 3: "Community Hospital Gamma" -- Medical Device Pivot
========================================================================

Date: Q4 2024
Organization: 150-bed community hospital, single location, ~600 staff
Sector: Acute care, small/rural

WHAT HAPPENED:
Attackers compromised an internet-facing patient portal that had an
unpatched web application vulnerability. The portal server was in a DMZ
but the DMZ configuration allowed outbound connections to the internal
network (a common misconfiguration). From the portal server, the attackers
discovered that medical IoT devices (patient monitors and infusion pumps)
were accessible on the same network as clinical workstations and servers.

The attackers did not initially target patient data. They used the
compromised portal to install cryptocurrency mining software on the portal
server and 3 clinical workstations. However, during their lateral movement,
they discovered that several infusion pump management interfaces were
accessible with default credentials. They accessed the pump management
console, which contained patient names and medication dosage information.

The breach was discovered by a biomedical engineering technician who noticed
unusual network traffic from the infusion pump network during routine
maintenance, 23 days after initial compromise.

WHY IT WORKED:
- The patient portal had a known web application vulnerability. The patch
  had been available for 2 months.
- The DMZ allowed outbound connections to the internal network. The firewall
  rules permitted this "for application functionality" but it defeated the
  purpose of the DMZ.
- No network segmentation between medical devices and the rest of the
  infrastructure. Once on the internal network, all IoT devices were
  directly reachable.
- Medical device management interfaces used default credentials. The
  infusion pump management console used vendor-default admin/admin
  credentials that had never been changed.
- No network monitoring detected the lateral movement or the crypto-mining
  traffic for 23 days. The only detection was a human noticing unusual
  traffic patterns manually.
- Medical device firmware had known vulnerabilities (vendor had issued
  advisories recommending network isolation as the primary mitigation since
  firmware patches were not available).

IMPACT:
- Patient data exposure for approximately 800 patients (names, medications,
  dosage data from infusion pump management system)
- Crypto-mining caused performance degradation on 3 clinical workstations,
  potentially affecting care delivery
- FDA notification required due to medical device involvement
- 23-day dwell time with complete network access
- $420,000 in incident response and remediation costs
- Forced network redesign and medical device isolation project ($180,000)

LESSONS CITED IN PUBLIC REPORT:
1. Medical IoT devices MUST be on isolated network segments
2. Default credentials on any system are an open door
3. DMZ misconfiguration (allowing outbound to internal) is as dangerous as
   having no DMZ
4. Network monitoring is the difference between a 23-day breach and a
   same-day detection
5. Medical device security requires collaboration between IT, security and
   biomedical engineering
