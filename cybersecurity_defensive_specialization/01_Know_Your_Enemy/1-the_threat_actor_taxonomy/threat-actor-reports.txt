========================================================================
REPORT A
========================================================================

A global pharmaceutical company discovered that proprietary drug trial
data had been systematically copied from its research servers over a
period of 14 months. The attackers used a custom-built remote access
tool that communicated via encrypted DNS queries, making detection
extremely difficult. The malware was signed with a stolen code-signing
certificate. Forensic analysis revealed that the initial compromise
occurred through a zero-day vulnerability in the company's VPN appliance.
The stolen data included Phase III clinical trial results valued at
an estimated $2 billion in future revenue.

========================================================================
REPORT B
========================================================================

A regional hospital's billing department was targeted by an email
campaign. The emails contained invoices that appeared to come from a
medical supply vendor. When opened, the attached PDF exploited a known
Adobe Reader vulnerability to install a commercially available
remote access trojan (RAT). Three weeks later, ransomware was deployed
across the hospital network. Before encryption, approximately 15GB of
patient records were exfiltrated to an external server. The attackers
demanded 40 Bitcoin (approximately $1.6 million) and threatened to
publish the patient data if payment was not received within 72 hours.

========================================================================
REPORT C
========================================================================

A hospital's public website was defaced overnight. The homepage was
replaced with a message criticizing the hospital's recent decision to
close its free community health clinic. The message included the logo
of a known activist group and called for protests. No patient data was
accessed. The defacement was achieved through a SQL injection
vulnerability in the website's content management system. The attackers
made no attempt to move beyond the web server to internal systems.

========================================================================
REPORT D
========================================================================

An IT administrator at a healthcare insurance company was terminated
after a disciplinary hearing. Two days later, production database tables
containing claims processing data were dropped (deleted). Investigation
revealed the administrator had created a secondary VPN account before
termination that was not linked to the corporate directory. The account
was used at 2:30 AM from the administrator's home IP address. The
administrator had also disabled the automated backup job for the claims
database 3 days before being fired.

========================================================================
REPORT E
========================================================================

A clinic network discovered cryptocurrency mining software on 12 of
its workstations. The software had been installed via an automated
exploit targeting a known vulnerability in a remote management tool
(CVE published 6 months earlier). The attackers did not attempt to
access patient data, move laterally to servers, or install persistent
backdoors. The mining software was a publicly available tool configured
to mine Monero. The attacker's wallet address was linked to a cluster
of similar infections across 300+ organizations worldwide, suggesting
mass automated exploitation rather than targeted attack.

========================================================================
REPORT F
========================================================================

A hospital network's nurse call system began malfunctioning during a
weekend. Investigation revealed that an employee in the biomedical
engineering department had connected a personal Raspberry Pi to the
medical device network "to monitor network performance for a personal
project." The Pi was running an outdated version of Raspbian with
default credentials (pi/raspberry). An external attacker had discovered
the device through an internet-facing port that the employee had
inadvertently exposed, used the default credentials to gain access,
and pivoted from the Pi to the nurse call system. The employee had
no malicious intent.

========================================================================
REPORT G
========================================================================

A mid-size hospital discovered that 3,200 patient records had been
accessed and downloaded over a 6-week period. The access occurred
through a legitimate physician account, always during off-hours
(between 11 PM and 4 AM), and always from the same IP address. The
physician in question had been on extended medical leave for the
entire period. When confronted, the physician denied any involvement
and produced documentation showing they were out of the country.

The downloaded records did not correspond to any of the physician's
current or former patients. The records were concentrated in patients
with high-value insurance plans. No ransom demand was received. The
data has not appeared on known dark web marketplaces as of the time
of this report.

========================================================================
REPORT H
========================================================================

A healthcare technology startup received an email from an unknown
sender claiming to have discovered a critical vulnerability in their
patient scheduling API. The sender demanded $50,000 in cryptocurrency
in exchange for not publishing the vulnerability details and the
2,000 patient records they had extracted as proof. The sender provided
a sample of 50 records that were verified as authentic. Analysis of
the API logs confirmed unauthorized access from a Tor exit node. The
vulnerability exploited was a broken authentication endpoint that had
been reported internally by a junior developer 3 months earlier but
was deprioritized in the development backlog.
