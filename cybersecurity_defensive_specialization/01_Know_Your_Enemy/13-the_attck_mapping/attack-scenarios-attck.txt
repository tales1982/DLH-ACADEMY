SCENARIO ALPHA: "Operation Flatline" -- Ransomware Campaign
========================================================================

Threat Actor: BlackReef affiliate (organized crime / RaaS)
Target: MedDefense Health Systems
Objective: Double extortion (data theft + encryption)

--- NARRATIVE ---

STEP 1:
A BlackReef affiliate purchases a list of healthcare organizations with
Fortinet VPN appliances from an initial access broker on a dark web forum.
The list was compiled by scanning the internet for FortiGate management
interfaces and cross-referencing with healthcare industry databases. The
affiliate pays $2,500 for a list of 40 organizations, including
MedDefense.

STEP 2:
The affiliate crafts a spear phishing email targeting Sarah Park
(IT Director, MedDefense). The email appears to come from Fortinet
support and warns of a critical firmware vulnerability. It includes
a link to a fake Fortinet support portal that hosts a malicious
document. Sarah downloads and opens the document on her workstation
(WS-HQ-01 at Corporate HQ). A macro executes a PowerShell command
that downloads and runs a reverse shell payload.

STEP 3:
The reverse shell connects to the affiliate's command and control
server. The affiliate now has a foothold on Sarah's workstation
(10.10.20.10) at Corporate HQ. They install a persistent backdoor
by creating a scheduled task that re-establishes the connection
every 30 minutes, disguised as a Windows Update task.

STEP 4:
From Sarah's workstation, the affiliate runs network discovery
commands: "nltest /dclist:", "net group 'Domain Admins' /domain",
and "arp -a" to map the network. Because HQ connects to Central via
site-to-site VPN and the network is flat, the affiliate can see the
entire 10.10.0.0/16 range. They identify ad-dc-01 (10.10.2.20),
ehr-srv-01 (10.10.2.10), ehr-db-01 (10.10.2.11), billing-srv-01
(10.10.2.15) and backup-srv-01 (10.10.2.40) / NAS-01 (10.10.2.41).

STEP 5:
The affiliate uses Mimikatz on Sarah's workstation to dump cached
credentials from memory. Sarah's account has local admin rights on
her workstation but not domain admin rights. However, the credential
dump reveals that a domain admin account (svc_backup) was used to
authenticate on this machine during a recent backup troubleshooting
session. Its NTLM hash is captured.

STEP 6:
Using the svc_backup hash, the affiliate performs a pass-the-hash
attack to authenticate to ad-dc-01 (10.10.2.20). They now have
Domain Admin access to the entire MedDefense Active Directory
environment. They verify access by querying the domain for all
computer objects.

STEP 7:
Before deploying ransomware, the affiliate locates high-value data.
They connect to ehr-db-01 (10.10.2.11:5432) -- the PostgreSQL
database is accessible from anywhere on the network with no
additional authentication beyond OS-level access. They use pg_dump
to export the patient database (approximately 35 GB). They also
access file-srv-01 for HR and financial documents (8 GB). The data
is compressed and exfiltrated to an attacker-controlled cloud
storage account using Rclone over HTTPS. No alerts are generated.

STEP 8:
The affiliate targets the backup infrastructure. NAS-01 (10.10.2.41)
is on the same network and accessible via its web interface
(port 5001). Using the domain admin credentials, they access the
NAS management interface and delete all backup jobs and stored
backups. They also delete Volume Shadow Copies on all Windows
systems using "vssadmin delete shadows /all /quiet".

STEP 9:
The affiliate creates a Group Policy Object on ad-dc-01 that
deploys the BlackReef ransomware payload to all domain-joined
Windows systems at the next Group Policy refresh cycle (within
90 minutes). The ransomware encrypts all local drives and accessible
network shares. Linux servers (ehr-srv-01, billing-srv-01) are
targeted separately via SSH using credentials found in a
configuration file on file-srv-01.

--- END SCENARIO ALPHA ---


========================================================================
SCENARIO BETA: "The Quiet Departure" -- Insider Data Theft
========================================================================

Threat Actor: Malicious insider (billing department employee)
Target: Patient records and financial data
Objective: Data theft for financial gain (selling records)

--- NARRATIVE ---

STEP 1:
Maria, a billing department employee at MedDefense Central, learns
that she will be laid off as part of a cost-reduction initiative.
She has 3 weeks before her last day. Maria has been with MedDefense
for 4 years and has legitimate access to the billing system
(billing-srv-01) and the EHR system (read-only access for billing
verification purposes). She decides to copy patient records to sell
on the black market.

STEP 2:
Maria begins by assessing what data she can access through her
normal work applications. Through the billing interface, she can
view patient names, dates of birth, insurance information, diagnosis
codes and billing amounts. Through her EHR read-only access, she
can view medical histories and prescription records. She notes that
the EHR application does not limit the number of records she can
view in a session or alert on unusual access volumes.

STEP 3:
Over the next two weeks, Maria accesses approximately 200 patient
records per day during her normal work hours, mixed in with her
legitimate work. She uses the EHR's built-in export function to
download CSV files of patient data to her workstation. The export
function is available to all users with read access -- no additional
authorization is required. No alert is triggered because the
EHR audit log records the access but nobody reviews the logs.

STEP 4:
Maria transfers the CSV files from her workstation to a personal
USB drive. No Group Policy restricts USB storage devices on
MedDefense workstations. She plugs in the drive at her desk without
concern because she has seen other employees use USB drives for
personal purposes without consequence. Over 2 weeks, she accumulates
approximately 2,800 patient records.

STEP 5:
To cover her tracks, Maria deletes the CSV files from her
workstation and empties the recycle bin. She does not know that the
EHR application maintains its own access audit log on ehr-srv-01,
which she cannot access or modify. However, as Marcus noted, these
logs require a 48-hour vendor export request and are never reviewed
proactively.

STEP 6:
On her second-to-last day, Maria notices that the billing application
stores database connection credentials in a configuration file on
her workstation (a practice she discovered months ago). She copies
this file to her USB drive as well, giving her direct database
access credentials for billing-srv-01 even after her network access
is revoked.

STEP 7:
Maria's last day arrives. HR processes her departure. Her manager
submits a ticket to IT requesting account deactivation. The ticket
sits in the queue for 5 business days before being processed (IT
has no SLA for offboarding tickets and no automated deactivation
linked to HR termination). During these 5 days, Maria's VPN
credentials remain active.

STEP 8:
Three days after her last day, Maria connects to the MedDefense VPN
from home using her still-active credentials. She accesses
billing-srv-01 using the database credentials she saved and extracts
an additional 400 records directly from the database. She then
disconnects and does not access the network again.

--- END SCENARIO BETA ---
