================================================================================
          CFO PUSHBACK -- ROBERT KIM'S OBJECTIONS
          MedDefense Health Systems
          Context: Board meeting preparation, reviewing security budget
================================================================================

Robert Kim has reviewed the proposed $120,000 security investment and
the supporting cost-benefit analysis. He has 5 objections.

========================================================================
OBJECTION 1: "We have never been breached. Why spend $120,000 now?"
========================================================================

"MedDefense has operated for 12 years without a significant security
incident. Yes, there was the crypto-miner on the billing server, but
that was a nuisance, not a breach. Our patient data has never been
compromised. I understand the theoretical risk, but $120,000 is real
money that could fund two additional nursing positions or upgrade our
cardiac monitoring equipment. Can you demonstrate that we WILL be
breached, not just that we COULD be?"

========================================================================
OBJECTION 2: "Your ALE numbers are estimates, not facts."
========================================================================

"I have reviewed the Annualized Loss Expectancy calculations. You
estimate the ransomware ALE at approximately $300,000. But that number
depends on assumptions: the ARO of 0.25 (once every 4 years), the
exposure factor of 60%, the asset value including reputation damage.
Change any assumption by 30% and the number changes dramatically. How
can I justify a real $120,000 expenditure based on estimated losses
that could be off by a factor of two?"

========================================================================
OBJECTION 3: "Insurance is cheaper than controls."
========================================================================

"We currently carry a cyber insurance policy with a $1 million
aggregate limit and a $50,000 deductible. The annual premium is
$38,000. If we are breached, insurance covers the majority of the
financial loss. Why should we spend an additional $120,000 on
prevention when insurance covers the consequences for a third of the
cost? I am not saying we should not invest in security at all, but
the insurance already provides financial protection."

========================================================================
OBJECTION 4: "This should be IT's regular budget, not a special ask."
========================================================================

"Sarah Park's IT department already has a $1.2 million annual budget
for infrastructure, support, and operations. Security should be part
of that existing budget, not a separate line item. If security is a
priority, Sarah should reallocate from her current budget. Every
department wants a special budget. If I approve $120,000 for security,
the facilities manager will want $120,000 for building upgrades, and
the HR director will want $120,000 for a new training platform."

========================================================================
OBJECTION 5: "Can we start with $60,000 and see if it works?"
========================================================================

"I understand the risk analysis, but the Board is conservative. They
approved the security analyst hire (your position) as a first step.
What if we fund $60,000 this year, see measurable results, and then
request the remaining $60,000 next year? This is standard practice
for new programs. Prove the concept, then scale. What can you deliver
for half the requested budget?"

========================================================================
          END OF CFO OBJECTIONS
