# 0. The Framework Landscape — MedDefense Health Systems

**Analyst:** Security Analyst
**Date:** Current

## Part 1: Three-Framework Summary

### NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0

Published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology, a U.S. federal agency, NIST CSF 2.0 (released February 2024) is a voluntary, outcome-based framework designed to help any organization — regardless of sector or size — understand, manage and communicate its cybersecurity risk. Its primary purpose is strategic: it does not tell you which specific tool to buy or which exact setting to configure, it tells you *what outcomes* a mature security program should achieve. It is structured as six Functions (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover), broken down into 22 Categories and 106 Subcategories, each describing a specific desired outcome rather than a prescriptive technical step. CSF is most commonly used by executives, risk managers and CISOs to build a common vocabulary between technical teams and the Board, and to structure a "Current Profile vs. Target Profile" gap analysis — exactly the exercise this project performs in Task 1.

### CIS Controls v8

Published by the Center for Internet Security, a nonprofit organization, CIS Controls v8 is a prioritized, prescriptive set of defensive actions distilled from real-world attack data, designed to answer the practical question CSF deliberately leaves open: *specifically what do I implement, and in what order?* It is structured as 18 Controls (e.g., Inventory of Enterprise Assets, Account Management, Continuous Vulnerability Management), each broken into concrete Safeguards, organized into three cumulative Implementation Groups — IG1 (56 safeguards, essential hygiene for every organization), IG2 (+74 safeguards, 130 total, for organizations with more complex environments and dedicated staff), and IG3 (+23 safeguards, 153 total, for organizations facing sophisticated or nation-state-level threats). CIS Controls are most commonly used by IT and security operations teams as an implementation checklist, and by smaller or resource-constrained organizations as a de facto starting framework because IG1 alone is explicitly designed to be achievable without a large security department.

### ISO/IEC 27001:2022

Published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) jointly with the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), ISO 27001 is an international, certifiable standard for building and operating an Information Security Management System (ISMS) — a management *system*, not a technical checklist. Its primary purpose is governance and demonstrable assurance: an organization can be independently audited and formally certified against it, which is something neither NIST CSF nor CIS Controls offer on their own. It is structured in two parts: Clauses 4–10, which define the mandatory management-system requirements (context, leadership, planning, support, operation, performance evaluation, improvement), and Annex A, which lists 93 candidate controls across four themes (Organizational — 37, People — 8, Physical — 14, Technological — 34) that an organization selects from based on its own risk assessment. ISO 27001 is most commonly used by organizations that need to *prove* their security posture to external parties — customers, regulators, insurers or business partners — through a formal, third-party-audited certificate.

## Part 2: Relationship Map

These three frameworks are not competing standards fighting for the same job — they operate at three different altitudes and are frequently used together, precisely because each one deliberately leaves gaps the others fill. NIST CSF operates at the strategic altitude: it tells an organization *what* outcomes to pursue (e.g., "vulnerabilities are identified, prioritized and remediated") without dictating the mechanism, which makes it ideal for Board-level conversation and for structuring a gap analysis like the one this project builds. CIS Controls operates at the operational altitude: it takes a CSF outcome like the one above and hands an implementer the *how* — Control 7's specific safeguards on vulnerability management process, remediation timelines and automated patching — turning a strategic goal into a checklist an IT team can actually execute. ISO 27001 operates at the assurance altitude: it doesn't compete with either one's content so much as it wraps a *management system* around whatever controls an organization has chosen (which could be CIS Controls, NIST SP 800-53, or a custom set) and provides the formal process — risk assessment methodology, management review, internal audit, continual improvement — plus a certificate that lets MedDefense *prove* to a regulator or auditor that the outcomes CSF describes and the safeguards CIS prescribes are actually being managed, not just implemented once and forgotten. In practice, a mature organization commonly uses all three together: NIST CSF to set the target profile and talk to the Board, CIS Controls (particularly IG1/IG2) as the concrete implementation roadmap, and ISO 27001's management-system discipline (or a lighter, uncertified version of it) to keep the whole thing running and auditable over time.

## Part 3: MedDefense Framework Selection

**Recommendation: adopt NIST CSF 2.0 as the strategic backbone, with CIS Controls v8 (IG1, then IG2) as the operational implementation layer. Defer formal ISO 27001 certification.**

MedDefense is a regional hospital with one security analyst and one Deputy CISO — not a federal agency with a compliance mandate for NIST SP 800-53/RMF, and not yet an organization with the staffing depth or program maturity that a full ISO 27001 certification effort assumes (ISO certification typically requires a dedicated management-system owner, a documented internal audit function, and a multi-month certification audit cycle — none of which MedDefense currently has bandwidth for, per Sarah Park's own admission that the organization follows "no framework formally"). NIST CSF 2.0 is free, requires no certification body, and its Govern/Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover structure maps directly onto exactly the three projects of work already completed (1x00 mapped Identify and parts of Protect, 1x01 informed Govern's risk-management-strategy and Identify's risk-assessment categories, 1x02 stress-tested Protect and exposed Detect as essentially absent) — meaning MedDefense does not start this framework adoption from zero, it starts from a Current Profile that already has three projects of evidence behind it. CIS Controls v8, layered underneath CSF, gives James and Sarah's two-person team something CSF alone cannot: a concrete, prioritized, IG1-first checklist that does not require a large staff to execute meaningfully — this is precisely the profile CIS Controls IG1 was designed for. **Recommendation for later:** once CSF/CIS Controls have been operating for 12–18 months and the organization can demonstrate consistent execution, MedDefense should revisit ISO 27001 certification specifically as a market differentiator and regulator/insurer confidence signal, but pursuing it now — before the underlying controls it would certify even exist — would be certifying a program that isn't there yet.
